Bloom's taxonomy

Bloom's Taxonomy is a hierarchical framework used in education to classify educational learning objectives into levels of complexity and specificity. Developed by educational psychologist Benjamin Bloom in the 1950s, it has become a fundamental tool for teachers and educators.


The taxonomy is divided into three main domains: cognitive, affective, and psychomotor. The cognitive domain focuses on intellectual skills and knowledge acquisition, ranging from basic recall to complex problem-solving. The affective domain deals with emotions, attitudes, and values, emphasizing the development of personal and social skills. The psychomotor domain relates to physical skills and coordination, encompassing tasks that require physical manipulation and control. Bloom's Taxonomy encourages a progressive approach to learning, moving from lower-order thinking skills (remembering, understanding) to higher-order skills (applying, analyzing, evaluating, creating). Educators use it to design effective learning experiences, assess students' understanding, and promote critical thinking and creativity. The taxonomy's versatility makes it applicable across various educational levels and subjects, providing a structured framework for curriculum development and assessment strategies.
